Research on Fetal Deaths

Chapter 5 Results

5.1 Fetal Deaths Over Years

First of all, let us take a look at how the fetal death rates changed between 2005 and 2019. We can clearly see a decreasing trend since 2007. Since we don’t have data before 2007, even though our graphs show there might be an increasing trend before 2007, we cannot simply make a conclusion. After further checking the movement by splitting the data into different genders, we found out that from 2007 to 2008, the female fetal death rate had a sharper decrease than male data did. We should notice that our graph shows higher death rates for male fetuses. This is not because male fetuses have a higher possibility of death but because male fetuses have higher birth rates in general. This decreasing trend is a good indicator, showing an improvement in national health. It is also unsurprising: with the development of technology and medical skills, we can now detect any potential factors that lead to fetus deaths more efficiently.

5.2 Geographical Distribution

From the graph above, we can clearly see the contribution of different US states to the total number of US fetal deaths. We found that CA, TX, FL, GA, PA, NY and IL accounted for the largest share of total fetal deaths in the United States. The states with the highest number of fetal deaths are mostly along the coast. However, some northern states (ex. MT, ND, ME, VT…) have very low numbers of fetal deaths.

5.3 Fetal Characteristics

5.3.1 Infant Delivery Weights

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), the average weight at birth for a full-term baby boy is 3,300 grams (g). The average full-term baby girl weighs 3,200 g, at birth. The average baby born at 37 to 40 weeks weighs between 2,500 to 4,000 grams. Birth weights less than 2,500 grams will be considered low birth weights.

From the figure above, we can see that fetuses with a birth weight of 500-999 grams and fetuses with a birth weight of 350-499 grams died the most. The number of deaths of fetuses with birth weights in these two ranges was much higher than for any other birth weight. Not only that, we can also see that the top number of deaths of fetuses were under 2500 grams of birth weight. So we can conclude that underweight fetuses have a higher mortality rate. However, the data shows that the number of fetal deaths outside the average range of fetal weight is low.

5.3.2 Plurality of Birth

In this part, we analyze whether the plurality of birth affects the number of fetal death. The above plot is a violin plot. The violin plot effectively shows not only median, range and variability, but also data distribution and its probability density. In the chart above, we can see that the single births have the widest distribution of fetal deaths and the highest number of fetal deaths.

5.3.3 Infant Delivery Method

We plotted the relationship between different delivery methods, the number of fetuses per birth and the number of fetal deaths. Above is a Mosaic plot. From the graph above, we can clearly see that the number of fetal deaths caused by cesarean is much lower than that caused by vaginal delivery, no matter the fetus is single, twin, triplet or more. Moreover, singletons were the most likely to die, regardless of delivery method.

5.3.4 Gestational Age

We believe that another factor determining whether a fetus will survive in good health is the gestational age of the mother. So we plotted the percentage of fetal deaths to the total fetal deaths at different gestational ages. In the bar chart above, we can see that the highest number of fetal deaths occur in gestational aged 20-23 weeks. The odds of surviving fetuses were higher for gestational age over 40 weeks. The data provided by the World Health Organization can also verify our results. According to the data provided by the World Health Organization, we know that delivery within 28 weeks requires modern medical technology, otherwise the survival rate of the fetus is very low or is zero.

5.4 Maternal/Paternal Characteristics

We believe that fetal death is also related to the age of the mother and the age of the father. So we plotted the number of fetal deaths caused by different ages of the mother and different ages of the father. We found that when the age of the mother and the age of the father are both between 25 and 29, the number of fetal deaths is the highest. Another age group with a high number of fetal deaths was mothers and fathers both between 30 and 34 years old.

5.4.1 The Number of Fetal Deaths by Different Materanl Race and Materanl Marital Status

In this section, we aim to investigate the effect of the mother’s race and the mother’s marital status on the number of fetal deaths. We plotted the two independent variables, the mother’s race and the mother’s marriage. In Cleveland plot above, we can see that first, white mothers had the highest number of fetal deaths, and American Indian or Alaska Native mothers had the lowest number of fetal deaths. For whites and Asians or Pacific Islanders, the number of fetal deaths was higher for married mothers than for unmarried mothers. The other two groups had more deaths when their mothers were unmarried.
